Advice from students


Anonymous no.2

Engage in class and always remember to link to the wider world + society and/or the author's purpose for both unfamiliar and external essays. Comparatively, notes are not that important but always remember a few SPECIFIC film techniques in important scenes for your external question.

12English-W


  • This course is Year 12 English writing.
  • By taking this course you will be mainly learning how to analyze different texts, you will also be crafting your own texts including an oral text.
  • Future course opportunities this course provides are 13ENG & 13ENGW.

Advice from students


Anonymous no.16

Always read the book/watch the movie-- it's a lot easier to remember things if you've actually read/watched what's required. Practice writing essays, practice while being timed (I like to do 20-minute paragraphs with breaks in between), practice making essay plans, practice writing without looking at notes/quotes, practice integrating quotes seamlessly, and practice writing well-structured paragraphs (using PEDAL, etc). Memorise lines that you can use no matter what the question is-- e.g. for your 'link to society' part of the explanation of quotes. Definitely make a shared notes/quotes document with your friends for the film and novel (if you're in the same class, of course). Memorise quotes by covering them up and saying them out loud; don't worry if you don't get it right word for word in the exam since the marker probably won't realize (unless it's a very popular line). For the writing portfolio, make sure it's concise and every single sentence and word is necessary. Proofread and peer-edit with friends/classmates as much as possible. For the unfamiliar, always plan your answers first and memorize the recommended structure for answering. It's also helpful to read good essays (from exemplars etc) for both unfamiliar/essays and analyze how they got good marks-- take note of their structure, length, vocabulary, etc.
